Ranch History

The Outfits I Rode For

You don't learn the West from a book. You learn it from the back of a horse at 5am, from the smell of desert sage after a hard rain, from men who taught by doing and expected you to keep up. These are the outfits that shaped Powell Parker.

Diamond A
Ranch

Diamond A Ranch — Arizona

One of the legendary outfits in Arizona ranching history. Working cattle on the Diamond A wasn't just a job — it was a graduate school in horsemanship, livestock, and the kind of grit that doesn't wash off. Powell learned the land the hard way and wouldn't trade a day of it.

Arizona Ranching
Babbitt
Ranches

Babbitt Ranches — Northern Arizona

Babbitt Ranches is Arizona ranching royalty — one of the oldest and largest cattle operations in the state, running across millions of acres of high desert. Powell purchased horses from Babbitt and worked alongside cowboys who carried on a tradition that goes back generations. Good horses and better people.

Don Donnelly Dude Ranch — Gold Canyon, AZ

In the early 90s Powell worked as a wrangler at the Don Donnelly Dude Ranch, set against the rugged backdrop of the Superstition Mountains in Gold Canyon, Arizona. He gathered cattle alongside Don Donnelly himself at Butcher Vanderworth Ranch — a registered Longhorn operation — and shared trail stories with the legendary actor Ben Johnson at Twin Tanks behind Apacheland Movie Ranch. Country that gets in your blood and stays there.

The Arena

Competing in the Great States of
Arizona, Texas & California

Rodeo isn't a sport — it's a way of life. Powell competed across three states in two of the most demanding events in Western competition, putting his skills on the line in front of judges, crowds, and cattle that don't cooperate on command.

🤠

Bulldogging

Steer wrestling — or bulldogging as the old-timers call it — requires explosive horsepower, perfect timing, and the willingness to throw yourself off a running horse onto a steer twice your size. Powell competed and placed across Arizona, Texas, and California. Not for the faint of heart.

🪢

Team Roping

Header and heeler working in perfect sync — team roping is as much about communication as it is about skill. Powell competed as a header, reading cattle and making the throw that sets his partner up. A sport that rewards practice, horsemanship, and trust between horse and rider.

🌵

Three States, One Life

Arizona. Texas. California. Powell competed across the Southwest and West Coast, carrying his West Texas roots into every arena he entered. The competition was real, the friendships were lasting, and the lessons — about horses, cattle, and character — have never left him.

His Horse

Monty — The Rope Horse

Every serious cowboy has that one horse. The one that knows what you're thinking before you ask. For Powell Parker, that horse is Monty — an Appendix Quarter Horse bred for the head box, built for speed, and trained with the kind of patience that only comes from years in the saddle.


Appendix Quarter Horses carry the speed and refinement of the Thoroughbred with the athleticism and cow sense of the Quarter Horse. In the team roping arena, that combination is as close to perfect as it gets. Monty isn't just a horse — he's a partner.

Hollywood Called

Wrangler — Film & Commercials
Aired Internationally

When production companies needed real cowboys for film and commercial work, they came to Arizona — and Powell Parker answered the call. Working as a professional wrangler, Powell supplied horses, handled livestock on set, and brought the kind of authentic Western presence that you simply cannot fake.

“You can dress an actor up in boots and a hat. You can't give him thirty years in the saddle. That's what they were hiring — the real thing.”

His work appeared in productions aired internationally — commercials and film that needed the backdrop of the Arizona desert and the credibility of genuine horsemanship behind the camera. It was a chapter that proved what anyone who knows Powell already knew: the man is the real deal, and the camera doesn't lie.


The connection to Don Donnelly's operation and the Apacheland Movie Ranch — where generations of Western films were shot and where legends like Ben Johnson shared stories around the fire — put Powell at the intersection of authentic cowboy culture and the storytelling tradition of the American West.

Life Outside the Arena

The Adventure Never Stops

When you grow up in West Texas and come of age in the Arizona desert, the outdoors isn't a hobby — it's a language. Powell Parker speaks it fluently. From the rivers and lakes to the trails and the rodeo arena, the adventure is constant and the stories keep coming.

🎣

Fishing With Cristina

Some of Powell's best days don't involve a saddle — they involve a fishing rod and Cristina by his side. Whether it's early morning on a quiet Arizona lake or chasing bass somewhere the cell signal disappears, fishing is where Powell recharges. Not because it's easy. Because it's honest. Just you, the water, and whatever the day brings.

🥾

Hiking the Desert & Beyond

Arizona is one of the most breathtaking places on earth when you leave the pavement behind. Powell hits the trails regularly — Superstition Wilderness, the Tonto National Forest, and wherever the terrain gets interesting. The same grit that got him through a rodeo arena gets him up the trail before sunrise. Movement is medicine and the desert has always been his therapy.

🤠

Still Competing — Rodeo Life

The arena never really lets you go. Powell still competes in team roping across Arizona and beyond — Monty under him, the clock ticking, the crowd watching. It's the same rush it was in his Queen Creek days, and it means just as much. Rodeo isn't something Powell used to do. It's something Powell is. Past tense doesn't apply here.

“The best days of my life have either started before sunup on horseback or ended with a fishing line in the water and someone I love sitting next to me. That's not a bad way to measure a life.”

— Powell Parker
